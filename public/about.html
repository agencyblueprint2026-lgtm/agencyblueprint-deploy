01 — Origin

42 years of making
things by hand.

I'm a realism portrait artist. 42 years of that. Work sold across multiple continents. The kind of discipline where a millimetre matters, where a wrong mark can take hours to correct, where you don't get to hide behind approximation.

That background shapes everything I do. Products have to earn their place. Systems have to be honest. Craftsmanship isn't a marketing word — it's a standard that either holds or it doesn't.

Alongside the art I built a brand — Urban Outlaw Supply Co. — creating custom laser-etched kraft notebooks and EDC products. I designed the website. I built the e-commerce system. I handled the product photography, the copy, the logistics, the marketing. Entirely solo.

At some point that stops being sustainable. Not because you're not capable — but because you're spending creative energy on work that isn't creative.

02 — The Problem

The guides that existed
weren't written for me.

When I started exploring AI agents, the resources fell into two camps. There were the YouTube tutorials — fast, shallow, already outdated by the time you finished watching. And there were the technical deep-dives — Python repositories, API documentation, architecture diagrams that assumed you'd spent years in engineering.

"Most resources assume you know Python. They assume you can debug JSON errors. Most professionals cannot. This guide exists for the majority."

I'm not in either camp. I understand systems. I understand logic. I understand what outcome I'm trying to achieve and I can describe it precisely. But I'm not a developer and I never needed to be.

The gap — between "I understand what I need" and "I know how to build it" — was enormous. And no one had written the bridge honestly.

03 — The Shift

What actually
changed things.

The shift wasn't a single moment. It was a slow accumulation of delegated tasks that used to eat hours.

Stage 01 — Chat
Started using Claude for copywriting, product descriptions, and brand voice. Fast wins, but still manual. I was the bottleneck between the tool and the output.
Stage 02 — Workflow
Connected tools. Built simple automations. Began understanding triggers, outputs, data mapping. The work started moving without me pushing it.
Stage 03 — Agency
The agents started making decisions. Classifying. Routing. Drafting. The distinction between automation and agency became real — not theoretical. I stopped operating the system. I started supervising it.
Stage 04 — Leverage
Creative energy returned to creative work. The repetitive, structured, time-consuming tasks had a place to go. This is what the Blueprint is built to give you.
04 — Principles

What this
is built on.

These aren't values statements. They're the structural beliefs that shaped every chapter of the Blueprint.

01 —
Practitioners over theorists
Everything in the guide was tested on real work, for a real business. If it doesn't function under actual conditions, it doesn't go in.
02 —
Outcomes before tools
The question is never "what can this do?" It's "what do I need done?" Tools are secondary. Clarity of goal is primary.
03 —
Start small. Build one thing.
Overbuilding is the most common failure mode. One working agent that you understand beats five half-built systems you can't maintain.
04 —
Failure is structural
Every agent will fail eventually. Designing for that — with guardrails, error handling, draft modes — is not pessimism. It's competence.
05 —
Judgment stays human
Automate the repetitive. Preserve the irreversible for human decision. Agents support thinking. They don't replace it.
06 —
Honesty over hype
Agents hallucinate. Tools break. Costs escalate if you're not watching. None of that is hidden here. The Blueprint tells you what works and what to watch for.
05 — Location

Cambridge,
England.

Based in Cambridge — a city where rigorous thinking is ambient, where the gap between research and application is unusually short, and where the audience for "intelligent systems explained plainly" turns out to be larger than you'd expect.

Researchers. Academics. Founders. Independent professionals who understand complexity but have no interest in becoming engineers. That's the room this was written for.

Portsmouth by origin. 42 years of craft by training. Cambridge by choice. The Agency Blueprint by necessity — because the guide I needed didn't exist, so I wrote it.
